# Mind / Body / Soul expansion — expert review & audit

_Date: 2026-06-16 · Branch: `feat/finance-garden`_

## Scope reviewed

This audit covers the content-and-gamification expansion of the "You Got This!" PWA:

- **Lessons:** 20 per Mind subject (Money, Parenting, Communication/NVC, Memory) — web-sourced and adversarially fact-checked.
- **Meditations:** Soul library expanded to 30, durations exact, trauma-aware.
- **Gamification:** 27 shared badges + 16 per-track badges (scholar / master / 7-day streak / a marquee topic badge per subject), plus cross-pillar balance badges.
- **Quality gates:** static validator at 1345/1345; full browser sweep with no console errors; service worker at `ygt-v4.0.0`.

## Method

A panel of seven expert dimensions reviewed the app in parallel; every critical/high finding was then handed to an independent adversarial verifier instructed to refute it. A lead auditor synthesized the verified results. Dimensions: Architecture & code efficiency, UX, product & competitive analysis, Content accuracy & attribution, Accessibility (a11y), Security & PII, Performance & runtime, Additivity, isolation & reward balance.

## Overall verdict

> Ship-ready and notably principled. The core invariants (count-based garden, learning isolation, grace-aware non-punitive streaks, local-only privacy) are sound and were verified in code. There are no critical or high-severity defects after adversarial review — every "high" was downgraded to medium because each degrades rather than blocks and has a designed fallback. The work needed before this feels like a finished, best-in-class product is concentrated in two areas: (1) the first-run experience (onboarding, in-flow name/coach capture, a guided first session, hiding "Coming soon" stubs), which is the single biggest competitive gap; and (2) a small batch of correctness/hygiene cleanups (service-worker precache completeness, dead-code removal, the reduced-motion override and route-focus accessibility fixes). None of these are blockers; all are well-scoped and most are low-effort.

## Executive summary

"You Got This!" is a privacy-first, client-only wellbeing PWA organized around three pillars — Body (move), Mind (learn), Soul (be still) — that feed one shared, count-based garden. Across six expert dimensions (architecture, UX/product, content accuracy, accessibility, security/PII, performance/runtime, and additivity/isolation) the app is in strong shape and largely free of the dark patterns common to habit software. Its two defining technical decisions both hold up under scrutiny: the finance module was correctly generalized into a trackId-parameterized learning engine (learning.js + learning-screen.js + a tracks.js registry), and the learning completion path deliberately bypasses recordSession (writing mins:0) so lessons can never inflate the minutes-based fitness levels or fitness badges. I independently verified the load-bearing claims in the working tree: the learning isolation invariant (mins:0, no recordSession), the orphaned finance.js/finance-screen.js (referenced only in comments), the avatar dependency chain absent from the service-worker precache (GLTFLoader.js, BufferGeometryUtils.js, vera.glb all exist on disk but are not in PRECACHE under the v4.0.0 cache bump), and the two reduced-motion override gaps (confetti.js and both avatars read only the OS media query; the in-app store.profile.reducedMotion setting is never threaded into animation code).

No critical or high-severity defects survived adversarial verification. The two findings originally rated "high" by the accessibility expert (focus never moved on route change; in-app Reduce Motion not reaching confetti/avatar) were both downgraded to medium on verification because the experiences degrade rather than block and a working OS-level fallback exists for motion. The originally "high" runtime finding (avatar chain missing from precache) was downgraded to medium because it affects only an opt-in beta with a designed lean-avatar fallback. The single genuinely highest-leverage gap is a product one, not a defect: there is no first-run onboarding or guided first session, which is a complete miss versus best-in-class incumbents at the most important moment in the funnel. Content accuracy is exemplary — every fact-bearing lesson is sourced to primary authorities, benefits are paired with honest caveats, and deep web verification of five sampled lessons confirmed the sources support the claims. Security holds the "private by design" promise: all data is local-only with no exfiltration path, output is consistently HTML-escaped, and no secrets are committed.

## Verified strengths

- Verified isolation invariant: the learning path (startLessonFor -> learningDone -> finishLearning/recordQuiz) writes mins:0 and deliberately bypasses recordSession, so lessons grow the shared garden by exactly +1 per action and can never touch totalMins/durationsTried/tiersTried/moveCounts/breathCloses or the minutes-based fitness levels and badges. Confirmed in js/learning.js (header comment lines 9-14, mins:0 at lines 144/192).
- Registry-driven architecture that is actually followed: tracks.js holds badge rules, lesson catalogs, props, themes, and quizzes as data; learning.js and learning-screen.js never hard-code a subject, so adding a Mind subject is a genuine 'drop in two modules + one descriptor'. Dependency graph is one-directional via location.hash with no circular imports.
- Best-in-class non-punitive, anti-compulsion loop: the garden grows on consistency not intensity, streaks are forgiven by an automatic and silent rolling grace-day rule (up to 2 misses per 7-day window), leaving a program claws nothing back, every move has a 'self-care, not failure' skip, and badges are set-if-unset and never revoked. This is White-Hat empowerment design done right.
- Exemplary content accuracy and attribution: every fact-bearing lesson carries a sources array of primary authorities (IRS, SEC/Investor.gov, CFPB, FDIC, Federal Reserve, AAP, CDC, NIH/NIA, Harvard, CNVC/Rosenberg, peer-reviewed psychology); benefits are paired with honest caveats; NVC is correctly attributed and honestly framed as 'one influential approach, not settled science'; deep web verification of five sampled lessons matched their sources exactly.

- Disciplined runtime lifecycle: player.js clears its interval in both dispose() and _finish() (guarded by an _finished flag) and removes its visibilitychange listener and wake lock on every path; both Three.js avatars fully dispose geometries/materials/textures, disconnect the ResizeObserver, pause rAF on tab-hide, and the photoreal avatar is kept off the default path via dynamic import.
- Strong accessibility foundation: every interactive control is a native focusable button/link, a robust global :focus-visible style is defined, narrated content always has aria-live captions plus a readable transcript, game/quiz progress is announced via role=status, and reduced-motion is honored at the CSS layer plus the OS media query path for confetti and avatar breathing.

## Prioritized findings

Severity and status are post-verification (`confirmed` / `partial` / `refuted` / `unverified`). No critical or high-severity **code** defect survived verification; the single `high` item is a product/UX gap, not a defect.

### [HIGH · confirmed] No first-run onboarding or guided first session

- **Dimension:** UX, product & competitive analysis
- **Recommendation:** This is the single highest-leverage gap and is a product opportunity, not a code defect; severity reflects competitive/conversion impact at the most important moment in the funnel. The router falls through to a fully populated home screen with no welcome, no in-flow name capture (name is buried in Settings, defaults to ''), no coach/encouragement choice, and no escorted first action. Add a short, skippable first-run flow gated on a seenOnboarding flag: one warm 'consistency not intensity' framing line; optional first-name capture; a one-tap coach + encouragement-style pick; and a single 'let us do one tiny thing right now' CTA that drops into the shortest Body/Soul session so the user earns garden stage 1 in their first minute. Keep it to 3 screens, fully skippable.

### [MEDIUM · partial] Realistic-avatar dependency chain (GLTFLoader.js, BufferGeometryUtils.js, vera.glb) missing from the SW precache

- **Dimension:** Performance & runtime
- **Recommendation:** Downgraded from high: the photoreal coach is an opt-in beta with a designed lean-avatar fallback, so the offline failure is silent but non-breaking. Verified: all three files exist on disk (vera.glb is 1.9 MB) and none appear in PRECACHE under the v4.0.0 cache bump; they are reached only via a lazy dynamic import, so a cold offline start fails. Add the two small JS loaders to PRECACHE and warm the 1.9 MB GLB separately via a best-effort cache.add() in install, wrapped so its failure cannot reject the atomic addAll().

### [MEDIUM · confirmed] In-app 'Reduce motion' setting does not reach confetti or avatar breathing

- **Dimension:** Accessibility (a11y)
- **Recommendation:** Downgraded from high: the OS prefers-reduced-motion path is fully honored and the affected animations are short-lived and decorative; the gap harms only users who rely on the in-app toggle without an OS preference. Verified: confetti.js (line 7) and both avatars (avatar.js:397, realistic-avatar.js:113) read only window.matchMedia; store.profile.reducedMotion is referenced only in main.js and state.js. Add one helper returning true when reducedMotion==='on' OR ('auto' AND media query matches), and use it as the authoritative gate in confetti.js and both avatar breathing guards.

### [MEDIUM · confirmed] Focus is never moved to the new screen on route changes

- **Dimension:** Accessibility (a11y)
- **Recommendation:** Downgraded from high: degrades rather than blocks (focus is not trapped; content stays reachable by tabbing). Verified: render() scrolls to top but never moves focus, and the per-screen innerHTML-overwrite pattern repeats across main.js and learning-screen.js with no tabindex anywhere in js/ and no document.title updates; the only .focus() calls are the safety/birthday modal traps (main.js:516, 812). Centralize in render(): give each screen's primary heading tabindex=-1 and call heading.focus() after render, paired with a document.title update or aria-live announcement (WCAG 2.4.3, Level A).

### [MEDIUM · confirmed] Orphaned files js/finance.js and js/finance-screen.js are precached but never imported

- **Dimension:** Performance & runtime
- **Recommendation:** Verified: both files are referenced only in descriptive comments (learning.js, learning-screen.js, tracks.js); the live engine is learning.js + learning-screen.js + tracks.js. They are dead code listed in PRECACHE, so if either is ever deleted the atomic cache.addAll() fails and the app loses all offline capability. Remove both from PRECACHE and delete the files as a follow-up; this also trims two install-time fetches.

### [MEDIUM · unverified] Timed Blitz arcade game offers no pause, extend, or disable-timer option

- **Dimension:** Accessibility (a11y)
- **Recommendation:** buildBlitz() in games.shared.js runs a fixed setInterval countdown that force-finishes at 0, with no accommodation (WCAG 2.2.1 Timing Adjustable). Users with low vision, motor, or cognitive disabilities, or anyone reading prompts via screen reader, cannot reasonably complete it. Provide at least one accommodation: a Pause/Resume control, the ability to extend time, or an untimed mode (low cost since Blitz shares the quiz data).

### [MEDIUM · unverified] Empty close-move pool causes a crash via unguarded dereference in sessionEngine

- **Dimension:** Architecture & code efficiency
- **Recommendation:** In buildSession (js/sessionEngine.js ~103-114), shuffle(closeChoices)[0] and the arrival move are dereferenced for .id without a guard; if a tier+category filter ever leaves zero close/arrival moves, this throws a TypeError that aborts session construction in the one module with no try/catch (planFor does not wrap buildSession). Non-reproducible with current data but one config change away. Add a guard: if closePool/arrivalPool is empty, fall back to the unfiltered EXERCISES set or return null so planFor's existing !plan path sends the user to the chooser.

### [MEDIUM · unverified] Several small-text color tokens are near or below the 4.5:1 contrast minimum

- **Dimension:** Accessibility (a11y)
- **Recommendation:** --finance-accent #5B6BD0 on white sits at ~4.7:1 (no margin), the gold source pill (--finance-gold-deep #9A6500 on --sun-200 #FFF1C9 at 0.68rem/800) is borderline, and --ink-soft #3D6B4C is used pervasively for small hints/captions (WCAG 1.4.3). A code comment already acknowledges coral/teal subject accents fail and remaps only those. Verify each small-text token against its real background and darken the failing ones (e.g. use --finance-ink for money's small accent text, matching the parenting/communication treatment).

### [MEDIUM · unverified] Gated tier selection and destructive actions rely on native alert()/confirm() with no programmatic association

- **Dimension:** Accessibility (a11y)
- **Recommendation:** Locked tier cards use aria-disabled but surface the gate reason via window.alert with no aria-describedby link between the button and its gate text; reset and end-session also use window.confirm (main.js ~344-353, 649, 1169). Associate each locked tier button with its gate message via aria-describedby, and replace alert()/confirm() with the existing accessible role=dialog overlay pattern already used for the safety and birthday modals (WCAG 4.1.3, 3.3.1).

### [MEDIUM · unverified] Visible 'Coming soon' stubs make the product feel unfinished

- **Dimension:** UX, product & competitive analysis
- **Recommendation:** The Mind pillar renders disabled greyed cards for unregistered subjects and Soul hardcodes 'Crystal energy' and 'Dream interpretation' stubs (main.js ~244-277). Advertising empty rooms erodes first-impression trust. Hide not-yet-live subjects entirely (or consolidate into a single subtle 'more growing here soon' line) and surface a subject only once its track is registered.

### [LOW · unverified] Third-party TTS model dynamically imported from jsDelivr without SRI

- **Dimension:** Security & PII
- **Recommendation:** natural-voice.js hardcodes the kokoro-js@1.2.1 +esm URL and await import()s it, executing third-party JS in the app origin with full localStorage access; dynamic import() cannot carry an integrity hash. Triggers only after explicit opt-in to the natural-voice beta, and the version is pinned. Per the project's own static-site runtime-dependency rule, prefer self-hosting the bundle and model under lib/, or at minimum add a CSP allowlisting only the jsDelivr/HuggingFace origins and document the third-party execution in the opt-in copy.

### [LOW · unverified] No Content-Security-Policy delivered by the application

- **Dimension:** Security & PII
- **Recommendation:** index.html ships no CSP and there is no header config in the repo, while the app uses innerHTML heavily. Current injection is well-mitigated by esc()/textContent, so this is defense-in-depth, not an active vulnerability. Add a restrictive CSP (default-src 'self'; script-src 'self' https://cdn.jsdelivr.net; connect-src 'self' plus the model-host origins; object-src 'none'; base-uri 'none'; frame-ancestors 'none') and verify the natural-voice flow still loads under it.

### [LOW · unverified] Two large data modules (meditation.js 228 KB, lessons.js 103 KB) are on the synchronous startup path

- **Dimension:** Performance & runtime
- **Recommendation:** main.js statically imports meditation.js, and tracks.js pulls in lessons.js, so ~330 KB+ of JSON-shaped JS is parsed/evaluated on every cold start even for a user who only does a movement session. The per-item builders are on-demand, so this is parse/eval cost that grows linearly as the catalogs expand. Move the heavy catalog bodies behind dynamic import() (as realistic-avatar.js already is), keeping only a lightweight index on the eager path; re-measure before/after.

### [LOW · unverified] Dead imports, write-only state, and an unread plan flag

- **Dimension:** Architecture & code efficiency
- **Recommendation:** LEVELS is imported in main.js (line 11) but never used; store.profile.defaultTier is written in two router branches (with a redundant save() each) but never read; isLesson:true is set on lesson plans but never consumed. Remove the LEVELS import, either wire defaultTier into the intended re-entry UX or delete it plus its two writes, and drop the isLesson flag or document its future consumer.

### [LOW · unverified] Persistent global QA handle (window.__nrjf) leaks player/store/avatar references

- **Dimension:** Architecture & code efficiency
- **Recommendation:** Every session assigns window.__nrjf = { player, store, avatar } (main.js:641) and it is never cleared on teardown, so a disposed Player and old avatar stay reachable and the full mutable store is exposed on window in production. Gate it behind the existing ?dev= flag or null it out in teardownSession().

### [LOW · unverified] Utility helpers (esc, date formatters) duplicated across modules

- **Dimension:** Architecture & code efficiency
- **Recommendation:** esc() and a toLocaleDateString formatter are independently defined in both main.js and learning-screen.js. esc() is the security-relevant HTML-escape primitive, so divergence risks an inconsistent XSS-relevant fix. Promote both into a small shared util module and import in both places.

## Per-dimension detail

### Architecture & code efficiency

The eight files form a clean, well-reasoned registry-driven PWA. The standout architectural decision — generalizing the old finance module into a trackId-parameterized learning engine (learning.js) + shared hub/completion UI (learning-screen.js) + a data registry (tracks.js) — is correctly executed: the engine and UI never hard-code a subject, navigation is one-directional via location.hash so there are no circular imports, and the learning path is deliberately isolated from the fitness/meditation scoring path (mins:0, no recordSession) to prevent inflating minute-based levels. ES-module hygiene is generally strong: explicit named imports, no leaked globals except one intentional QA handle. The issues found are real but mostly low-to-medium severity: a few dead imports/flags/state fields, duplicated utility helpers (esc, date formatters) across files, repeated badge-card rendering blocks, and one genuine correctness fragility in sessionEngine where the close move is dereferenced without a guard if its pool is empty. No O(n^2)-over-session-array problems of concern at realistic data sizes, though several screens do full re-renders and re-scans on each interaction.

**Strengths**

- Registry-driven design (tracks.js) is the right abstraction and is followed consistently: learning.js and learning-screen.js read badge rules, lesson catalogs, props, themes and quizzes as data, so adding a Mind subject is genuinely 'drop in two modules + one descriptor'. The vacuous-truth guard in checkTrackBadges (need.length > 0 before need.every) shows real care about misconfiguration.
- Strong separation of concerns and isolation: learning completions push exactly ONE shared garden/streak record with mins:0 and deliberately bypass recordSession so totalMins/durationsTried/tiersTried/moveCounts/breathCloses can never be touched by a lesson. The reasoning is documented inline and the code matches it (learning.js finishLearning/recordQuiz).
- Clean one-directional dependency graph: learning-screen.js imports only data/logic modules and navigates by setting location.hash, with main.js owning the router and avatar/Player wiring. This avoids the circular-import trap that this kind of screen<->router split usually creates.
- Player lifecycle and teardown are careful: teardownSession()/dispose() clear the interval, remove the visibilitychange listener, release the wake lock, cancel TTS and dispose the avatar; _finish() is idempotent via the _finished guard; renderSeq guards against a stale async import resuming after navigation.
- Defensive, resilient runtime behavior: WebGL avatar creation is wrapped in try/catch with a graceful no-webgl fallback and a mid-session lean-avatar swap on a fresh canvas (a disposed GL context cannot rebind — correctly handled); wake-lock and sfx calls are individually try/caught; safeUrl() only ever emits http(s) hrefs as defense-in-depth even though sources are currently constant.
- Output is consistently HTML-escaped via esc() at interpolation points, and the streak engine (streakInfo) is a thoughtful, well-commented grace-day algorithm bounded to a 730-iteration walk.

**Findings**

- **[medium]** Empty close-move pool causes a crash via unguarded dereference — closePool = pool.filter(blocks.includes('close')); closeChoices derived from it; then `const close = shuffle(closeChoices)[0]; used.add(close.id);`. If the tier+category filter leaves zero 'close' moves (or zero arrival moves at line ~113-114, where `arrival` can be undefined), close/arrival is undefined and `.id` throws a TypeError that aborts session construction. tierAllows/categoryAllows always pass close+breath moves, so today the pool is non-empty — but this is a single data/config change away from a hard crash, in the one module with no try/catch around it (the caller planFor() does not wrap buildSession).. _Rec:_ Add an explicit guard after computing closePool/arrivalPool: if either is empty, either fall back to the unfiltered EXERCISES close/arrival set or return null so planFor()'s existing `if (!plan)` path sends the user back to the chooser. At minimum, assert and log so a misconfiguration fails loudly rather than as an opaque TypeError.
- **[low]** Dead imports, write-only state, and an unread plan flag — (1) `LEVELS` is imported in main.js line 11 but never referenced anywhere in the file (only levelInfo is used). (2) `store.profile.defaultTier` is defaulted in state.js and written in two router branches (`store.profile.defaultTier = tier; save();`) but never read by any module — confirmed by grep; it is write-only state plus a redundant save() on every play route. (3) `isLesson: true` is set on lesson plans in lessons.shared.js and lessons.js but no code ever reads `plan.isLesson` (playback keys off `isMeditation`/`kind`).. _Rec:_ Remove the unused `LEVELS` import. Either wire `defaultTier` into the re-entry UX it was intended for, or delete the field and both writes (removing two needless save() calls). Drop the `isLesson` flag or document the intended future consumer; dead flags invite incorrect assumptions.
- **[low]** Utility helpers duplicated across modules instead of shared — esc() is independently defined in main.js and learning-screen.js with identical escape maps. A date formatter with the same `new Date(iso).toLocaleDateString(undefined,{year,month,day})` + try/catch body exists as fmtDateL in main.js and fmtDate in learning-screen.js. These are the app's security-relevant HTML-escape primitive and a display helper — divergence risk if one is patched (e.g. to escape backticks) and the other is not.. _Rec:_ Promote esc() and the date formatter into a small shared util module (e.g. js/util.js or extend state.js's surface) and import in both places. Consolidating the escape function in particular reduces the chance of an inconsistent XSS-relevant fix.
- **[low]** Badge-card / celebration rendering duplicated four times — The same pattern — `result.earned.map(id => { const b = allBadges.find(x=>x.id===id); return b ? `<div class="badge-pop">...` : ''; }).join('')` plus the `[...BADGES, ...allTrackBadges()]` lookup list — is repeated in three functions in learning-screen.js and a near-identical variant in main.js's finishSession. The `let finished=false` re-entrancy guard around the build callback is also copy-pasted between gameScreen and quizScreen.. _Rec:_ Extract a `renderBadgePops(earnedIds, allBadges)` helper (and an `allBadges()` accessor) into learning-screen.js, and a tiny `onceGuard(fn)` for the build callbacks. This removes ~40 lines of duplication and makes the badge-pop markup single-source.
- **[low]** Per-interaction full re-renders with O(n) session/lesson rescans — youScreen() rebuilds the entire activity log by iterating every track's full lessons array, pushing into `entries`, then sorting and slicing(0,10); logging a weight or saving a birthday calls youScreen() again, re-scanning and re-sorting from scratch and re-attaching all listeners. Similarly 'Play again'/'Try again' re-invoke gameScreen/quizScreen which fully replace innerHTML. Not O(n^2) and fine at expected data volume (lessons/sessions grow slowly), but it is unmemoized work plus listener churn on every small edit.. _Rec:_ This is acceptable at current scale — flag for awareness only. If the activity log or weights grow large, cap the per-track scan or maintain a precomputed recent-activity list rather than rebuilding and re-sorting all historical entries on each save.
- **[low]** Persistent global QA handle leaks player/store/avatar references — Every session assigns `window.__nrjf = { player, store, avatar }` and the swap path updates `window.__nrjf.avatar`. It is labeled a harmless local-only QA handle, but it is never cleared on teardownSession()/finishSession(), so a disposed Player and an old avatar stay reachable from a global for the life of the page (minor retained-memory / stale-reference surface), and it exposes the full mutable store on window in production.. _Rec:_ Gate the handle behind the existing dev flag (the `?dev=` path already exists) or null it out in teardownSession(). At minimum clear `window.__nrjf` when the session ends so it does not pin a disposed Player/avatar.
- **[info]** Broad app-level 'change' listener for the intake summary — refreshSummary() is wired to every change event bubbling to `app`, and it calls readIntake() which re-queries all `[data-parq]`/`[data-injury]` nodes and recomputes availableTiers on each change. The listener is attached to `app` (not a per-screen element); since app.innerHTML is replaced on the next render the handler is dropped with its nodes, so it does not accumulate — but it is a coarse-grained subscription doing a full intake re-read per keystroke/toggle.. _Rec:_ No action required for correctness. If tightening: scope the listener to the intake form section and/or debounce refreshSummary, so unrelated changes elsewhere in a future shared container cannot trigger a full intake recompute.

### UX, product & competitive analysis

"You Got This!" is a privacy-first, on-device wellness app organized around three pillars — Body (move), Mind (learn), Soul (be still) — that all feed one shared garden. It already embodies the single most important lesson from the best-loved habit apps: a non-punitive, identity-affirming reward loop. The garden grows on consistency not intensity; streaks are forgiven by a rolling grace-day rule (matching Headspace/Duolingo "streak freeze"); leaving a program is penalty-free; and the framing is investment-in-growth rather than fear-of-loss. This is genuinely additive and largely free of dark patterns — a notable achievement given how easily streaks slide into guilt. The principal gaps are not ethical but experiential: there is effectively no first-run onboarding (the app drops users straight onto a populated home screen with no name capture, no "pick one tiny thing" first win, and no expectation-setting narrative), the reward cadence is mid/long-grained (the garden only visibly changes at sessions 1, 3, 6, 10, 15, 21, 28, 36 — long droughts between stages 4–8), and the three-pillar IA, while elegant, leaves several Mind subjects and all "Soul" extras as visible "Coming soon" stubs that dilute the sense of a finished product. Against Headspace, Calm, Duolingo, YNAB/NerdWallet, and Finch/Fabulous, the app matches the best on ethics and privacy, trails them on the guided first session and the moment-to-moment reward drip. Note: file inspected read-only; no changes made. CSS for animation/timing values was skimmed, not exhaustively audited.

**Strengths**

- Best-in-class non-punitive loop. The core promise — 'consistency makes it bloom, never intensity' — is stated on the home screen, the pillar chooser, and inside every subject hub, and it is actually enforced in code: gardenStage/levels read only completed-session counts and minutes, never intensity (js/gamify.js, js/main.js:151). This is the White-Hat, empowerment-over-loss-aversion design the literature praises and that Finch is loved for.
- Streak grace done right. streakInfo() forgives a missed day as long as no rolling 7-day window has more than 2 misses, anchors the chain on the most recent active day, and surfaces a reassuring 'move today to keep it growing' nudge plus a soft 'streak safe' note (js/gamify.js:44-95, js/main.js:81-86). This is the Headspace/Duolingo 'streak freeze' pattern, but automatic and silent — no anxiety, no purchasable repair, no guilt.
- Penalty-free exits everywhere. Leaving a guided program explicitly claws nothing back ('completed sessions already grew the garden', js/main.js:486-488), every move has a skip button framed as 'self-care, not failure' (safetyHTML, js/main.js:501), and skipping is destigmatized in copy. This is the opposite of the loss-aversion trap.
- One shared garden as a unifying metaphor. A workout, a meditation, and a lesson all grow the same garden and feed the same streak (js/main.js:151, learning-screen.js:122). This is a strong, legible progress metaphor — Finch/Fabulous-grade — and it cleverly avoids fragmenting motivation across three separate trackers.
- Privacy as a first-class, repeated promise. 'Private by design' appears on home, the You dashboard, and settings, and is true in the architecture (localStorage only, state.js). Even the weight tracker and birthday never leave the device. This is a real differentiator versus account-required incumbents and builds the trust habit apps depend on.
- Genuinely delightful, well-paced celebration. The done screen stacks garden growth, level-up, and badge pops with confetti and a sparkle chime; learning uses a tiered party() (gentle / win / big win for a 100% quiz or subject completion) so the celebration scales to the achievement (learning-screen.js:24-31). The once-a-year birthday party with layered confetti and affirmations (js/main.js:784-820) is a memorable, warm surprise-and-delight moment few competitors attempt.
- Strong accessibility and calm-by-default posture. Reduced-motion is honored three ways (OS media query, a forced override, and a user setting at js/main.js:39-42 + css 85-92, 358-362), captions are always on even when voice is off, chair mode exists, ARIA roles/labels are used on the level bar, timer, progress dots, and modals have a focus trap. Music defaults OFF (iOS-safe). This matches Calm/Headspace's 'reduce stress on open' philosophy.
- Health-responsible gating without shame. The PAR-Q readiness screen gates only the most vigorous tier behind a 'check with your clinician' message while keeping gentler sessions and meditation open; intake data is explicitly 'never shown as a number, never a goal, never used to shame' (intakeScreen, js/main.js:376). This is more ethically careful than most fitness apps.

**Findings**

- **[high]** No first-run onboarding or guided first session — the single biggest gap vs. best-in-class — On first launch the router falls through to homeScreen() (js/main.js:1337), which renders a fully populated dashboard (empty garden, Level 1, pillar grid) with only a safety overlay. There is no welcome/expectation-setting screen, no name capture in the flow (name is buried in Settings, state.js default name:''), no 'choose one small thing to try now' first action, and no escorted first session. The hello screen in index.html is just a 'Warming up…' splash. Headspace and Duolingo both open with narrative framing then walk the user into a 3–5 min first session; Finch/Fabulous use onboarding to create 'main character' investment.. _Rec:_ Add a short, skippable first-run flow gated on a 'seenOnboarding' flag: (1) one warm welcome line that sets the 'consistency not intensity' expectation; (2) optional first-name capture (the coach already personalizes with it); (3) a single 'let us do one tiny thing right now' CTA that drops into the shortest Body/Soul session so the user earns garden stage 1 in their first minute. Keep it 3 screens max and fully skippable to preserve the low-friction feel. _(verifier: confirmed → high)_
- **[medium]** Reward cadence has long dead zones at higher garden stages — GARDEN_STAGE_SESSIONS = [0,1,3,6,10,15,21,28,36] (js/data/garden.js:188). Early growth is fast and satisfying (sessions 1, 2→3, 3→6), but from stage 5→8 the user must complete 6, then 7, then 8 sessions to see the garden visibly change. Levels (js/gamify.js:7-18) similarly widen (450→600→800 min). Between visible milestones the only per-session reward is the same confetti + 'X min moved' card, so committed users hit a motivational plateau exactly when they most need reinforcement — the opposite of the variable, frequent micro-rewards Duolingo/Finch use to sustain engagement.. _Rec:_ Introduce a lightweight per-session micro-reward that always advances something visible between big stages — e.g. small garden flourishes (a new bloom, a butterfly, color shift) on a finer-grained counter, or a 'watered N days' micro-meter on the garden card. Keep the 8 macro-stages as the headline metaphor but ensure every single finished session changes the picture in some small way.
- **[medium]** Visible 'Coming soon' stubs make the product feel unfinished — The Mind pillar renders disabled, greyed subject cards for any subject not yet registered (mindScreen, js/main.js:268-277 — parenting/communication/memory show 'Coming soon' unless live), and the Soul pillar hardcodes two 'Coming soon' tiles for 'Crystal energy' and 'Dream interpretation' (soulScreen, js/main.js:244-249). Best-in-class apps rarely advertise empty rooms; it signals incompleteness and can erode trust on first impression.. _Rec:_ Hide not-yet-live subjects/practices entirely (or move them to a single 'More growing here soon' affordance) rather than rendering individual disabled tiles. Surface a subject only once its track is registered. If the roadmap teaser is intentional, consolidate it into one subtle line rather than full-size locked cards.
- **[low]** Mind/Body/Soul mapping of 'Soul = meditation only' is slightly arbitrary and the labels lean abstract — The three pillars are clear and visually consistent (pillarsHTML, js/main.js:136-153), but 'Soul' currently equals meditation plus two esoteric stubs, while breathing/calm arguably also serve the 'Mind' load. The blurbs do real work ('Move', 'Learn', 'Be still'), yet a brand-new user must infer that 'Mind = structured lessons on money/parenting' rather than mindfulness — a reasonable point of confusion since most wellness apps put meditation under 'Mind'.. _Rec:_ Keep the three-pillar IA — it is elegant and the verb-led subtitles help — but consider strengthening the one-word labels with the verb on the card itself at first run, and validate the Mind=learning / Soul=stillness split with a couple of users, since it inverts the common Headspace/Calm convention where mindfulness lives under 'Mind'.
- **[low]** Name and core personalization are hidden in Settings, weakening early emotional investment — Coach choice, encouragement style (gentle/cheerleader/funny), and name all live only in settingsScreen (js/main.js:993-1045) and are never prompted during normal use. The home greeting degrades gracefully to 'Hi!' without a name, but the coach's name-driven personalization (a clear strength of the four-coach cast) is invisible to users who never open Settings.. _Rec:_ Surface a one-tap 'pick your coach + how you like to be encouraged' moment in the first-run flow (or a dismissible home card after session 1). This is cheap to add, deepens the Finch-style character investment, and immediately activates the personalization machinery already built.
- **[low]** Destructive and end-session actions rely on native confirm() dialogs — Ending a session, resetting all data, and leaving flows use window.confirm()/alert() (js/main.js:649, 1169, 348). These break the calm, branded aesthetic the rest of the app cultivates, are unstyleable, and on some platforms read as jarring system interruptions — at odds with the Headspace/Calm 'every transition is gentle' standard the app otherwise meets.. _Rec:_ Replace confirm()/alert() with the app's existing styled .overlay modal pattern (already used well for safety and birthday). This keeps tone consistent at exactly the emotionally loaded moments (quitting, resetting) where polish matters most.
- **[info]** Streak grace is silent and possibly too generous to teach the habit — The rolling rule forgives up to 2 misses per 7-day window and the streak count includes forgiven days (js/gamify.js:44-95). This is excellent for avoiding anxiety, but the forgiveness is largely invisible (only a soft 'streakSafe' micro-line when usedGraceRecently). Users may not learn that the app has their back, and a streak that survives 2 missed days of every 7 is loose enough that the number may not reflect a real daily habit.. _Rec:_ No change required — this is a deliberate and defensible ethical choice. Optionally make the grace mechanic legible the first time it triggers (a one-time 'we kept your streak — life happens 💚' note), which turns an invisible kindness into a trust-building moment without adding pressure.

### Content accuracy & attribution

I reviewed all four lesson files in full (js/data/lessons.js — 20 finance lessons; js/data/lessons.parenting.js — 11; js/data/lessons.communication.js — 20 NVC; js/data/lessons.memory.js — 20) and skimmed js/data/meditation.js (a benign breath-pacing runtime with no factual claims). The content is unusually strong: it is consistently sourced to authoritative bodies (IRS, SEC/Investor.gov, CFPB, FDIC, Federal Reserve; AAP, CDC, NIH/NIA, Harvard Center on the Developing Child; CNVC/PuddleDancer/Rosenberg; peer-reviewed psychology), benefits are reliably paired with honest caveats, hedging is appropriate (no over-promising), and safety framing is excellent (every NVC lesson carries an abuse/danger boundary plus 988 and the 1-800-799-7233 hotline; memory and parenting lessons repeatedly redirect to a doctor/pediatrician). NVC is correctly and repeatedly attributed to Marshall Rosenberg and the Center for Nonviolent Communication, and is honestly framed as "one influential approach, not settled science." DEEP-VERIFY via WebFetch/WebSearch confirmed the cited sources support the claims for all five sampled lessons: money credit-score-basics (CFPB en-315 — verbatim "prediction... how likely you are to pay a loan back on time," 300–850, multiple scores) and hsa-and-fsa (IRS Pub 969 — triple tax advantage, HDHP requirement, FSA employer-established/use-it-or-lose-it, FSA-blocks-HSA all confirmed); memory the-major-system (PubMed 9248820 — no immediate advantage, modest at 3 days, strong at 1 week, six-digit lock combinations: exact match) and sleep-and-memory (CDC About Sleep — "improve your attention and memory" confirmed; 7+ hours for adults); communication conflict-de-escalation (Gottman — four horsemen definitions, flooding as physiological state, contempt as "single greatest predictor of divorce," 20-minute break, and the four antidotes all confirmed). Secondary deep-checks corroborated the Dresler 2017 memory-palace numbers (≈26→62 words of 72, 4-month persistence) and the FTC/Lumosity $50M-suspended-to-$2M judgment with dementia/Alzheimer's claims. No critical or high-severity inaccuracies were found. The findings below are low-severity refinements: minor citation-resilience notes and a few small framing tightenings.

**Strengths**

- Source discipline is exemplary: every fact-bearing lesson carries a `sources` array of authoritative, mostly-primary references (IRS notices/publications by number, SEC Investor.gov, CFPB ask-cfpb pages, FDIC, Federal Reserve G.19/FAQs, AAP/HealthyChildren, CDC, NIA/NIH, peer-reviewed PMC/PubMed, CNVC/PuddleDancer), and year-changing figures carry explicit yearLabel tags for easy maintenance.
- Benefits are systematically paired with honest caveats throughout — e.g. compound growth 'cuts both ways' on debt; diversification framed as 'a seatbelt, not a force field'; HSA triple tax break tied to 'real out-of-pocket risk'; brain games 'fun, not a shortcut'; dollar-cost averaging 'a discipline for staying invested, not a shield against losing money.'
- Hedging is calibrated and non-promissory: 50/30/20 called 'a rule of thumb, not a law'; emergency-fund size 'depends on your situation'; NVC repeatedly 'cannot change or control anyone, and promises no particular outcome'; correlation-vs-causation explicitly flagged in attention-and-memory (Stanford multitasking) and chronic-stress claims.
- Safety framing is best-in-class: the communication module's disclaimer and every individual NVC lesson explicitly exclude abuse/coercion/danger, state 'your safety comes first,' and give 988 and the National Domestic Violence Hotline (1-800-799-7233); memory and parenting lessons reliably redirect medical/developmental concerns to a doctor or pediatrician.
- NVC attribution is correct and consistent: credited to clinical psychologist Marshall Rosenberg and the Center for Nonviolent Communication on essentially every lesson, with the OFNR four-part framework accurately described and honestly labeled 'one influential approach, not the only one and not settled science.'
- Numeric claims that were deep-verified were precise, not approximate: the major-system study's nuanced time course (no immediate gain, modest at 3 days, strong at 1 week), the Dresler 72-word/26→62 figures, and the FTC $50M-suspended-to-$2M structure all matched their sources exactly — a sign of genuine fact-checking rather than paraphrase-from-memory.
- Affect-labeling (Lieberman et al. 2007) is consistently presented as independent, separate support ('a separate finding, not proof that NVC works'), avoiding the common error of citing neuroscience as if it validated the whole framework.

**Findings**

- **[low]** CFPB source does not rank payment history and utilization as the 'two biggest levers' on a credit score — The lesson says 'the two biggest levers, paying on time and keeping balances low, are squarely in your hands.' WebFetch of the cited CFPB page (what-is-a-credit-score-en-315) confirms it lists bill-paying history and credit utilization among the factors, but it presents the factors as a non-ranked list and does not state these two are the largest. The 'biggest' ranking is a (defensible, FICO-consistent) editorial framing that the specific cited URL does not itself support.. _Rec:_ Either soften to 'two of the most important factors, and the ones most in your control,' or add a citation that explicitly weights factors (e.g. a FICO/CFPB source describing payment history as the most heavily weighted) so the 'biggest levers' claim is directly backed.
- **[low]** Several primary-source URLs (CNVC, FTC) are bot-blocked, creating silent-failure risk for any automated link checker — WebFetch returned HTTP 403 for https://www.cnvc.org/learn/what-is-nvc and both FTC Lumosity URLs, while WebSearch confirmed the identical FTC press-release URL is canonical and live. The citations are correct and authoritative; the 403s are anti-scraping, not broken links. This is not a content defect, but it means a CI link-validator that does plain GETs could flag valid sources as dead.. _Rec:_ No content change needed. If a link-health check exists in the validator/service-worker tooling, whitelist cnvc.org and ftc.gov (or check them with a browser user-agent) so genuine citations are not falsely reported as broken.
- **[info]** 'Lifelike voice' / TTS pronunciation depends on spelled-out numbers, but a few segments mix spelled-out and acronym-spacing conventions — The files' design intent (header comment in lessons.js) is that numbers are spelled out for natural TTS. This is followed well, but acronyms rely on space-separation ('F D I C', 'I R A', 'A P Y', 'C D C') and a few dense numeric passages (e.g. Roth phase-out ranges '$153,000–$168,000' rendered as words) are long enough that a listener may lose the thread. No factual error.. _Rec:_ Optional: spot-check the longest numeric segments in the actual TTS player for intelligibility; consider chunking the densest figure lists. Purely a usability/QA note, not an accuracy or safety issue.
- **[low]** Parenting 'serve and return / one million new neural connections per second' is stated as fact without the usual age qualifier — The lesson states 'In the earliest years, more than one million new neural connections form every second,' attributed to the Harvard Center on the Developing Child. The Harvard figure is genuine and correctly attributed, but the original framing is specifically about the first few years of life; the lesson's 'in the earliest years' mostly preserves this. The claim is widely cited yet is an estimate, and the surrounding text presents it without the soft hedging applied elsewhere in the corpus.. _Rec:_ Minor: add a light hedge consistent with the rest of the module (e.g. 'researchers estimate more than one million...') so the one strong unhedged neuroscience number matches the careful tone used for other research claims.
- **[low]** Federal Reserve credit-card APR figure is cited with two slightly different vintages across lessons — Both lessons cite Federal Reserve G.19 for an average credit-card rate of ~21%, but with different reference periods (Q4 2025 vs March 2026). G.19 is released with a lag and revised, so a 'March 2026' figure may not yet be the finalized series value depending on release timing; the two lessons also imply slightly different snapshots of the same ~21% number. Not a material inaccuracy (the ~21% level is stable and correctly hedged as 'around'), but the mismatched vintages are a consistency/maintainability snag.. _Rec:_ Standardize on a single confirmed G.19 release date across both lessons (and the file header, which says 'Q4 2025'), and confirm the chosen month's value is actually published, so future updates touch one canonical figure.

### Accessibility (a11y)

The Money Garden learning module is built on a solid accessibility foundation: every interactive element (lessons, games, quizzes, navigation, settings) is a real, keyboard-operable button or link; a strong global :focus-visible style is defined; narrated content always has live-region captions plus a readable transcript; and confetti/avatar motion is decorative and partially respects reduced-motion. The most significant gaps are (1) focus is never moved on route changes, so screen-reader and keyboard users lose their place every time a screen re-renders; (2) the in-app "Reduce motion" override does not reach the confetti or avatar-breathing code, which read only the OS media query — so a user who chooses Reduce in Settings (with no OS preference set) still receives full multi-burst confetti; (3) the timed Blitz arcade game has no pause/extend mechanism (WCAG 2.2.1 Timing Adjustable); and (4) several small-text color tokens sit near or below the 4.5:1 contrast threshold. None are blockers, but the focus-management and reduced-motion override gaps affect users on every session. Findings below cite exact files and lines.

**Strengths**

- Every interactive control is a native, focusable element: games (quiz/sort/order/blitz, match, sequence) and all hub/lesson/quiz/settings controls use real <button>/<a> with type="button", so keyboard operability and Enter/Space activation come for free (games.shared.js, games.memory.js, learning-screen.js).
- Strong, visible global focus indicator: css/style.css:79 defines :focus-visible { outline: 3px solid var(--ink); outline-offset: 2px } which meets WCAG 2.4.7 and the 2.2 Focus Appearance intent across the whole app.
- Narrated lessons and meditations are never audio-only: the session caption bubble is a polite live region (main.js:543, aria-live="polite") fed by coach.onCaption, and the completion screen offers a full <details> transcript of what the coach said (learning-screen.js:217-223) — satisfying captions/transcript expectations for time-based media.
- Game and quiz progress is announced to assistive tech: status/feedback paragraphs carry role="status" (games.shared.js:39,53,96,110,150; games.memory.js status nodes), so score, question number, and correct/incorrect feedback reach screen readers without stealing focus.
- Reduced-motion is taken seriously at the CSS layer: a global @media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce) block plus an explicit html[data-reduced-motion="on"] override neutralize transitions/animations (css/style.css:85-91, 359-363); confetti renders a static bloom instead of motion when the OS preference is set (confetti.js), and avatar breathing is suppressed under reduced motion (avatar.js:397, realistic-avatar.js:113).
- All output is HTML-escaped via esc() and emitted text is consistent; decorative emoji/icons are correctly marked aria-hidden="true" (e.g. fin-lib-ic, pillar-ic, fin-info, bday-emoji), and the decorative confetti canvas sets aria-hidden="true" — reducing screen-reader noise.
- Modal overlays (safety, birthday) are implemented as role="dialog" aria-modal="true" with an accessible label, move focus to the confirm button on open, and trap Tab on the single actionable control (main.js:506-526, 798-814).

**Findings**

- **[high]** Focus is never moved to the new screen on route changes — render() in main.js (lines 1240-1338) re-renders the entire view by overwriting app.innerHTML in each screen function and calls window.scrollTo(0,0), but never sets focus on the new content. The same pattern repeats inside learning-screen.js after each app.innerHTML assignment (trackHubScreen, learningDone, gameScreen/gameDone, quizScreen/quizDone). When the previously focused button is destroyed, focus falls back to <body>, so keyboard and screen-reader users are silently returned to the top of the DOM with no announcement of the new screen (e.g. navigating from a lesson card into the lesson, or from a completed quiz back to the hub).. _Rec:_ After each screen render, move focus to the new screen's primary heading. Give the per-screen <h1>/<h2> tabindex="-1" and call heading.focus() at the end of the render (or focus the <main> landmark). Centralize this in render() so every route benefits, and pair it with an aria-live region or document.title update so the screen change is announced. _(verifier: confirmed → medium)_
- **[high]** In-app "Reduce motion" setting does not reach confetti or avatar breathing — The app exposes a three-way motion control (auto/on/off) saved to store.profile.reducedMotion and applied as html[data-reduced-motion] by applyMotionPref() (main.js:39-42, intake select at main.js:408-410). However confetti.js gates its motion solely on window.matchMedia('(prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)').matches, and avatar.js:397 / realistic-avatar.js:113 do the same for the breathing loop. A user who sets "Reduce motion" in Settings but has no OS-level preference still receives the full multi-burst confetti party (party(tier) in learning-screen.js fires up to three stacked celebrate() bursts) and the animated avatar. The CSS data-reduced-motion="on" override cannot help because confetti is canvas-drawn in JS and the avatar animates via requestAnimationFrame, not CSS transitions.. _Rec:_ Make a single helper that returns true when EITHER store.profile.reducedMotion === 'on' OR (it is 'auto' AND the media query matches), and false when 'off'. Use it in confetti.js and the avatar breathing guards instead of reading matchMedia directly, so the in-app preference is authoritative. _(verifier: confirmed → medium)_
- **[medium]** Timed Blitz arcade game offers no way to pause, extend, or disable the timer — buildBlitz() in games.shared.js:205-270 runs a fixed countdown (config.seconds || 30) via setInterval that decrements every second and force-finishes at 0, with no pause, no extend, and no option to turn the limit off. Each prompt must be read and answered against the clock; users with low vision, motor, or cognitive disabilities, or anyone using a screen reader to read the prompt and options, cannot reasonably complete it. The other concept games (quiz/sort/order) are untimed, so this is isolated to Blitz.. _Rec:_ Provide at least one of the WCAG 2.2.1 accommodations: a Pause/Resume control on the HUD, the ability to extend time, or a setting to play Blitz untimed (it shares the quiz data, so an untimed mode is low-cost). At minimum, respect the reduced-motion / accessibility preference to lengthen or remove the limit.
- **[medium]** Gated tier selection communicates only via window.alert and aria-disabled — In tierScreen() (main.js:344-353) a locked tier card has aria-disabled="true" (good — it stays focusable) but activating it surfaces the gate reason through window.alert(...). The locked state itself is conveyed visually with opacity and a lock glyph (.tier-card.locked, css/style.css:334) and the gate text is in a <span class="tier-gate">, but there is no programmatic association (e.g. aria-describedby) between the button and its gate explanation, and alert() is a jarring, non-styled interruption. Reset and End-session confirmations also rely on window.confirm (main.js:649, 1169).. _Rec:_ Associate each locked tier button with its gate message via aria-describedby so the reason is read on focus, and replace alert()/confirm() with the existing accessible overlay dialog pattern (role="dialog", focus management) already used for the safety and birthday modals.

### Security & PII

Reviewed the requested files plus their dependency surface (natural-voice.js, realistic-avatar.js, games.shared.js, tracks.js, badges.js) in the "You Got This!" client-only PWA. The app's "private by design" claim holds: all user data (progress, weight, birthday, name, intake answers) lives only in localStorage and is never transmitted — there is no analytics, no accounts, no server, and no sendBeacon/XHR/WebSocket anywhere in js/. The findings below are low/medium hardening items, not active vulnerabilities; none contradict the privacy promise.

**Strengths**

The privacy claim in the UI is accurate.

**Findings**

- **[low]** Third-party TTS model loaded from jsDelivr CDN without Subresource Integrity, executed as a module — js/natural-voice.js:10 hardcodes KOKORO_ESM = 'https://cdn.jsdelivr.net/npm/kokoro-js@1.2.1/+esm' and js/natural-voice.js:56 does `await import(KOKORO_ESM)`, dynamically importing and executing third-party JavaScript in the app origin. ### Performance & runtime

The codebase is in good performance shape for a client-side PWA: timer and interval cleanup is disciplined (isConnected guards in games.shared.js, clearInterval on both dispose() and _finish() in player.js), both Three.js avatars have correct animation-loop lifecycle (rAF paused on tab hide, cancelled on stop, full geometry/material/texture disposal plus ResizeObserver.disconnect() and listener removal on dispose), and the photoreal avatar is correctly kept off the default path via dynamic import. Three issues warrant attention, all in the service-worker precache. First and most serious: the realistic-avatar dependency chain that the SW is meant to make offline-capable is incompletely precached — js/lib/jsm/loaders/GLTFLoader.js, lib/jsm/utils/BufferGeometryUtils.js, and the 1.9 MB models/vera.glb are reachable but absent from PRECACHE, so the photoreal coach silently fails offline. Second: js/finance.js and js/finance-screen.js are precached but orphaned — no module imports them, so they are stale paths that waste install-time fetches and, if missing on disk, would fail the entire all-or-nothing addAll(). Third: two large data modules (meditation.js at 228 KB, lessons.js at 103 KB) sit in the synchronous startup import graph, inflating eager parse/eval cost on cold start even when the user never opens those sections. None of these are memory leaks; the leak surface is well managed.

**Strengths**

- Timer hygiene in games.shared.js is exemplary: every late callback (setTimeout completion handlers, the blitz setInterval) is gated on root.isConnected, and the blitz interval both clears itself when the root detaches and is explicitly cleared in finish() — navigating away mid-game leaks nothing.
- player.js clears its 1 Hz setInterval in BOTH dispose() (navigation away) and _finish() (natural/early end), removes the visibilitychange listener in both paths, releases the wake lock, and guards _finish() with an _finished flag against double-invocation.
- Both Avatar and RealisticAvatar implement a complete teardown: dispose() stops the loop, removes the document visibilitychange listener, calls ResizeObserver.disconnect(), traverses the scene disposing geometries/materials (and textures via map.dispose() in the realistic one), and calls renderer.dispose() — no dangling GPU resources or listeners across route changes.
- Render-loop lifecycle is correct and battery-aware: requestAnimationFrame is single-flighted via the _raf guard (_resume() early-returns if already running), paused on document.hidden, and resumed only if _running — a backgrounded tab does not burn frames.
- Avatar.setCharacter() disposes the previous rig's geometry and materials before building a new one, so repeated character swaps do not accumulate GPU buffers.
- Both avatars clamp setPixelRatio to a max of 2 and keep scenes tiny (no shadows, low-poly primitives / single 28k-tri skinned mesh), which is the right mobile-first GPU budget.
- The expensive photoreal path is lazy: realistic-avatar.js (and therefore GLTFLoader + the 1.9 MB GLB) is pulled only through a dynamic import() in main.js gated behind a capability check, and an in-session fps watchdog persists a 'slow' verdict to downgrade future sessions — it deliberately avoids a jarring mid-session hot-swap.
- RealisticAvatar.watchPerformance correctly ignores backgrounded tabs (document.hidden aborts the sample) so throttled rAF cannot produce a false 'slow' verdict.
- The service worker is structurally sound: versioned cache (ygt-v4.0.0), old caches purged on activate, same-origin GET-only fetch handling, cache-first with a navigation fallback to index.html, and ignoreSearch matching.

### Additivity, isolation & reward balance

The core invariant holds. A learning lesson, game, or quiz grows the SHARED garden by exactly +1 per user action and never touches totalMins / durationsTried / tiersTried / moveCounts / breathCloses, because the learning completion path (startLessonFor -> learningDone -> finishLearning/recordQuiz) deliberately bypasses recordSession and the minutes/duration/move/time-of-day code path in finishSession. The new cross-pillar badges (balance-day, whole-self-day, balanced-week) classify Mind/Body/Soul correctly via the session's lessonIds array / kind and are reachable. The per-track scholar/mastery/streak-7 conditions are reachable and fair: every topicBadge id maps to a real curriculum lesson, scholar requires all 20 individually-launchable lessons, mastery additionally requires a reachable 100% quiz plus one game win, and streak-7 uses the same grace-day engine as the shared streak. The whole system stays count-based, grace-aware, and free of shame/goals. I found no miscount and no philosophy violation. The one item worth the author's awareness (by design, not a bug) is that a single multi-lesson study session writes N per-track ledger entries but only +1 garden record, so per-track lesson counters advance faster than the shared garden — intended "combined activity" behavior, but it means scholar/seven can be earned in fewer than seven calendar days/sessions.
